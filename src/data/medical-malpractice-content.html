Understanding Medical Malpractice in California

Your Rights, Our Fight - Comprehensive Legal Support for Medical Negligence Victims

Critical Information for Medical Malpractice Victims

Medical malpractice occurs when a healthcare provider's negligence causes harm to a patient. In California, you have the right to seek compensation for injuries caused by medical errors, misdiagnosis, surgical mistakes, medication errors, and more. Time is critical - California law requires filing within specific deadlines. Contact us immediately for a free case evaluation.

What Constitutes Medical Malpractice in California?

Medical malpractice in California occurs when a healthcare professional fails to provide the standard of care that a reasonably competent medical professional would have provided under similar circumstances. This negligence must directly cause injury or harm to the patient. The California Medical Board oversees physicians and investigates complaints, but victims need experienced legal representation to pursue compensation through the civil justice system.

Misdiagnosis & Delayed Diagnosis

One of the most common forms of medical malpractice, misdiagnosis can lead to delayed treatment, unnecessary procedures, or complete lack of treatment for serious conditions. Cancer misdiagnosis, stroke misidentification, and heart attack misdiagnosis are particularly devastating, often resulting in preventable death or permanent disability.

Surgical Errors

Surgical mistakes include wrong-site surgery, leaving surgical instruments inside the body, nerve damage, unnecessary surgery, and anesthesia errors. These "never events" should never occur but happen more frequently than most patients realize, often causing catastrophic injuries or death.

Medication Errors

Prescription mistakes, incorrect dosages, dangerous drug interactions, and administration errors can cause severe adverse reactions, organ damage, or death. Pharmacists, doctors, and nurses can all be held liable for medication-related malpractice.

Birth Injuries

Medical negligence during pregnancy, labor, or delivery can cause lifelong disabilities. Cerebral palsy, Erb's palsy, brain damage from oxygen deprivation, and other birth injuries often result from failure to monitor fetal distress, delayed C-sections, or improper use of delivery instruments.

Emergency Room Errors

ER mistakes include failure to diagnose heart attacks or strokes, premature discharge, inadequate testing, and delayed treatment. The fast-paced emergency room environment is no excuse for negligence that causes patient harm.

Hospital-Acquired Infections

Preventable infections like MRSA, sepsis, and surgical site infections often result from poor sanitation, inadequate sterilization, or failure to follow proper protocols. Hospitals can be held liable for systemic failures that lead to patient infections.

Understanding California's MICRA Laws and Damage Caps

‚¨áÔ∏è

The Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (MICRA) has governed California medical malpractice cases since 1975. Recent reforms in 2022 have significantly changed the landscape for victims:

New MICRA Damage Caps (Effective January 1, 2023)

  • Non-Death Cases: Starting at $350,000 in 2023, increasing by $40,000 annually until reaching $750,000 in 2033
  • Wrongful Death Cases: Starting at $500,000 in 2023, increasing by $50,000 annually until reaching $1,000,000 in 2033
  • After 2033: Both caps will increase by 2% annually for inflation
  • Multiple Defendants: Separate caps apply for healthcare providers, healthcare institutions, and unaffiliated providers

What MICRA Doesn't Cap

Economic damages remain unlimited, including:

  • Past and future medical expenses
  • Lost wages and loss of earning capacity
  • Cost of rehabilitation and therapy
  • Home modifications for disabilities
  • Long-term care costs

Important Timeline Alert

California's statute of limitations for medical malpractice is complex: You must file within 3 years of the injury OR 1 year from discovery of the injury, whichever comes first. For minors, special rules apply. Don't delay - contact us immediately to protect your rights.

Proving Medical Malpractice: The Four Essential Elements

‚¨áÔ∏è

To win a medical malpractice case in California, we must prove four critical elements:

1. Duty of Care (Doctor-Patient Relationship)

We must establish that a formal healthcare provider-patient relationship existed. This creates a legal duty for the provider to deliver competent medical care. This is typically straightforward when you've been treated by the provider.

2. Breach of Duty (Negligence)

The healthcare provider failed to meet the accepted standard of care. We prove this through expert testimony from medical professionals who testify about what a reasonably competent provider would have done differently. Common breaches include:

  • Failure to order necessary tests
  • Misinterpreting test results
  • Ignoring patient symptoms or medical history
  • Performing unnecessary procedures
  • Lack of informed consent

3. Causation

The provider's negligence directly caused your injury. This requires showing that your harm wouldn't have occurred if the provider had met the standard of care. Medical experts help establish this causal link.

4. Damages

You suffered actual harm requiring compensation. This includes physical injuries, emotional distress, financial losses, and reduced quality of life. Without provable damages, there's no valid malpractice claim.

Common Medical Conditions Involved in Malpractice Cases

Certain medical conditions appear frequently in malpractice claims due to their serious nature and the devastating consequences of medical errors. Understanding these high-risk areas helps patients recognize potential negligence:

Cancer Misdiagnosis

High Risk Time-Sensitive

Failure to diagnose cancer, especially breast, lung, colorectal, and skin cancers, can mean the difference between life and death. Delayed diagnosis allows cancer to progress to advanced stages, reducing survival rates and treatment options.

Heart Attack & Stroke

Emergency Critical

Misdiagnosis of heart attacks and strokes in emergency rooms is alarmingly common. Symptoms dismissed as anxiety, indigestion, or minor issues can lead to death or permanent disability when treatment is delayed.

Infections & Sepsis

Preventable Fatal

Failure to diagnose or properly treat infections can lead to sepsis, a life-threatening condition. Hospital-acquired infections and delayed antibiotic treatment cause thousands of preventable deaths annually.

Pregnancy Complications

Mother & Baby Lifelong Impact

Preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, placental abruption, and other pregnancy complications require careful monitoring. Failure to diagnose or treat these conditions can cause maternal death or permanent injury to mother and child.

Appendicitis

Common Urgent

Often misdiagnosed as gastroenteritis or other conditions, untreated appendicitis can lead to rupture, peritonitis, sepsis, and death. Particularly dangerous in children and pregnant women.

Blood Clots & Pulmonary Embolism

Silent Killer Preventable

Deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolisms are often missed, especially in younger patients. Failure to recognize risk factors or symptoms can be fatal.

Compensation Available in Medical Malpractice Cases

California law allows medical malpractice victims to recover both economic and non-economic damages. Understanding what compensation is available helps victims and families plan for the future:

Economic Damages (No Cap)

  • Medical Expenses: All past and future medical costs, including surgeries, medications, therapy, and medical equipment
  • Lost Income: Past lost wages and future loss of earning capacity
  • Rehabilitation Costs: Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy
  • Long-term Care: Nursing care, home health aides, assisted living facilities
  • Home Modifications: Wheelchair ramps, bathroom modifications, medical equipment
  • Transportation: Medical appointments, specialized vehicles

Non-Economic Damages (Subject to MICRA Cap)

  • Pain and Suffering: Physical pain and emotional distress
  • Loss of Enjoyment of Life: Inability to participate in activities you once enjoyed
  • Disfigurement: Scarring or permanent physical changes
  • Loss of Consortium: Impact on marital relationship
  • Emotional Distress: Anxiety, depression, PTSD
  • Loss of Companionship: In wrongful death cases

  • Enhanced Electronic Health Records Requirements: New standards for maintaining and sharing patient records to prevent errors
  • Telemedicine Malpractice Standards: Updated guidelines for virtual healthcare liability
  • COVID-19 Related Claims: Special considerations for pandemic-related medical care

Medical Malpractice FAQs

Answers to Your Most Important Questions

What is the statute of limitations for medical malpractice in California? +
In California, you generally have 3 years from the date of injury OR 1 year from the date you discovered (or should have discovered) the injury to file a medical malpractice lawsuit, whichever comes first. For minors under 6, you have 3 years or until the child's 8th birthday, whichever provides more time. Cases involving foreign objects left in the body have special rules. Don't wait - contact us immediately to ensure you don't miss critical deadlines.
How much is my medical malpractice case worth? +
Case values vary greatly depending on the severity of injuries, economic losses, and circumstances. Economic damages (medical bills, lost wages) are unlimited. Non-economic damages (pain and suffering) are capped under MICRA - currently $430,000 for injury cases and $600,000 for wrongful death in 2025, increasing annually. Cases involving permanent disability, disfigurement, or death typically result in higher settlements. We provide free case evaluations to estimate your potential recovery.
Do I need expert witnesses for my medical malpractice case? +
Yes, California law requires expert testimony in almost all medical malpractice cases. Medical experts must establish the standard of care, prove the provider breached that standard, and demonstrate causation between the breach and your injuries. We work with top medical experts across all specialties and handle all expert costs upfront - you don't pay unless we win.
Can I sue multiple parties for medical malpractice? +
Yes, multiple parties can be held liable. This may include doctors, nurses, hospitals, medical groups, and pharmaceutical companies. Under California's new MICRA laws, you can potentially recover separate damage caps from healthcare providers, healthcare institutions, and unaffiliated providers. We identify all liable parties to maximize your recovery.
What if the doctor admits they made a mistake? +
A doctor admitting error can strengthen your case, but it doesn't automatically prove malpractice. We still must show the error fell below the standard of care and caused your damages. Under California's new laws, expressions of sympathy or apology are generally not admissible as evidence, but factual admissions of fault may be used. Document any admissions and contact us immediately.
How long does a medical malpractice lawsuit take? +
Medical malpractice cases typically take 1-3 years from filing to resolution, though complex cases may take longer. Factors affecting timeline include case complexity, number of defendants, court schedules, and whether the case settles or goes to trial. We work efficiently to resolve your case as quickly as possible while maximizing your compensation. Some cases settle within months, while others require trial preparation.
What if I can't afford medical treatment for my injuries? +
We can help you access medical care even if you can't afford it upfront. We work with medical providers who offer treatment on a lien basis, meaning they wait for payment until your case settles. We can also help you navigate insurance options and connect you with appropriate specialists. Your health is the priority - we ensure you get necessary treatment while we fight for compensation.
Can I sue for misdiagnosis if I didn't suffer permanent harm? +
You can pursue a claim if misdiagnosis caused you damages, even if not permanent. This includes unnecessary treatments, surgeries, medications with side effects, emotional distress, lost wages, and medical expenses. The key is proving the misdiagnosis caused harm that wouldn't have occurred with proper diagnosis. Temporary injuries with significant impact on your life can still result in substantial compensation.
What's the difference between medical malpractice and medical negligence? +
In California law, these terms are often used interchangeably. Medical negligence refers to a healthcare provider's failure to meet the standard of care. Medical malpractice is the legal claim arising from that negligence. Technically, negligence becomes malpractice when it causes patient harm and gives rise to a legal claim for damages. Both require proving the provider breached their duty of care.
Can I sue if my surgery had complications? +
Not all surgical complications constitute malpractice. Surgery carries inherent risks, and bad outcomes can occur even with perfect care. However, you may have a case if complications resulted from surgical errors like wrong-site surgery, leaving instruments inside you, damaging organs or nerves, or failing to monitor post-operative complications. We evaluate whether your complications were preventable negligence or unavoidable risks.
What if I signed a consent form? +
Signing a consent form doesn't waive your right to sue for malpractice. Consent forms acknowledge risks of properly performed procedures, not negligent care. You can still sue if the provider deviated from the standard of care, performed the wrong procedure, or failed to disclose material risks. Consent must be truly informed - if critical risks weren't explained, the consent may be invalid.
Can I sue an emergency room for misdiagnosis? +
Yes, emergency rooms and their staff can be held liable for misdiagnosis. While ERs are fast-paced, they must still meet the standard of care for emergency medicine. Common ER malpractice includes missing heart attacks, strokes, appendicitis, or serious infections. Premature discharge without proper testing is another frequent issue. The standard considers the emergency setting but still requires competent care.
What if the malpractice happened at a government hospital? +
Claims against government hospitals (county, state, or federal facilities) have special rules and shorter deadlines. You typically must file a government tort claim within 6 months of the injury before filing a lawsuit. Federal facilities require following the Federal Tort Claims Act procedures. These cases are complex - contact us immediately as missing government claim deadlines can bar your case entirely.
Can I sue for birth injuries to my child? +
Yes, parents can sue for birth injuries caused by medical negligence during pregnancy, labor, or delivery. Common birth injury claims include cerebral palsy from oxygen deprivation, brachial plexus injuries from improper delivery techniques, and brain damage from failure to perform timely C-sections. Both the child and parents may have separate claims for damages. These cases often involve lifetime care costs requiring aggressive representation.
What is informed consent in medical malpractice? +
California law requires doctors to obtain informed consent before treatment. This means explaining the procedure, its risks and benefits, alternatives, and consequences of refusing treatment. Failure to obtain proper informed consent can be malpractice if an undisclosed risk materializes and causes harm. The doctor must disclose risks a reasonable patient would want to know when deciding about treatment.
Can I sue a pharmacist for medication errors? +
Yes, pharmacists can be held liable for medication errors including dispensing wrong medications, incorrect dosages, failing to check for drug interactions, or not providing proper warnings. Pharmacies can also be liable for systemic failures. These errors can cause serious harm including allergic reactions, organ damage, or death. Both the individual pharmacist and pharmacy corporation may be liable.
What if my loved one died from medical malpractice? +
California allows two types of claims when malpractice causes death: wrongful death claims (for family members' losses) and survival actions (for the deceased's pain and suffering before death). Eligible family members include spouses, children, and sometimes parents or siblings. Wrongful death claims have higher MICRA caps ($600,000 in 2025) and can recover funeral expenses, loss of financial support, and loss of companionship.
Can I sue if I got an infection in the hospital? +
Hospital-acquired infections may be grounds for malpractice if caused by negligence such as poor sanitation, failure to follow infection control protocols, contaminated equipment, or inadequate sterilization. Not all infections are preventable, but many result from substandard care. Common actionable infections include MRSA, C. diff, surgical site infections, and catheter-associated infections. We investigate whether proper protocols were followed.
What if I can't get my medical records? +
California law requires healthcare providers to maintain and provide medical records upon request. If you're having difficulty obtaining records, we can help. We use legal tools including subpoenas to compel production of all relevant records. Suspicious alterations, missing records, or refusal to provide records can actually strengthen your case by suggesting consciousness of guilt. Never let record issues prevent you from pursuing justice.
Do I have to go to court for a medical malpractice case? +
Most medical malpractice cases (over 90%) settle without trial. However, we prepare every case as if it's going to trial to maximize leverage in negotiations. If court becomes necessary, we'll thoroughly prepare you for depositions and testimony. We handle all legal proceedings while keeping you informed. Our trial experience often leads to better settlements as defendants know we're willing to fight in court.
Can I sue for emotional distress from medical malpractice? +
Yes, emotional distress is compensable in medical malpractice cases. This includes anxiety, depression, PTSD, sleep disturbances, and other psychological impacts. Emotional distress damages fall under non-economic damages subject to MICRA caps. In some cases, family members witnessing malpractice may also have claims for emotional distress. Psychological counseling costs are economic damages not subject to caps.
What if the doctor is no longer practicing? +
You can still sue a doctor who has retired, moved, or lost their license. Malpractice insurance typically covers claims for past acts even after the doctor stops practicing. If the doctor has died, claims can proceed against their estate. The hospital or medical group may also be liable. Don't assume you can't recover compensation just because the doctor is no longer practicing.
Can I sue for failure to diagnose cancer? +
Failure to diagnose cancer is one of the most common and devastating forms of malpractice. Delayed diagnosis allows cancer to progress, reducing survival rates and requiring more aggressive treatment. We pursue claims for failure to order appropriate screening tests, misreading pathology or imaging, ignoring symptoms, or failing to follow up on abnormal results. Early detection saves lives - delays can be fatal.
What is the discovery rule in medical malpractice? +
California's discovery rule extends the statute of limitations when injuries aren't immediately apparent. You have one year from when you discovered or should have discovered the injury and its negligent cause. However, this cannot exceed the 3-year absolute deadline from the date of malpractice (with limited exceptions). The discovery rule often applies to missed diagnoses, surgical errors discovered later, or slowly manifesting injuries.
Can I sue a dentist for malpractice? +
Yes, dentists can be sued for dental malpractice under the same legal principles as medical malpractice. Common dental malpractice includes nerve damage during procedures, failure to diagnose oral cancer or infections, unnecessary tooth extractions, improper crown or implant placement, and anesthesia errors. Dental malpractice is subject to the same MICRA caps and legal requirements as medical malpractice.
What if I was injured by defective medical equipment? +
Defective medical devices may involve both malpractice and product liability claims. The healthcare provider may be liable for using defective equipment or improper use, while manufacturers can be liable for design or manufacturing defects. Common cases involve defective implants, surgical instruments, monitoring equipment, or diagnostic devices. These cases often allow recovery beyond MICRA caps through product liability claims.
Can I sue for anesthesia errors? +
Anesthesia errors can cause brain damage, heart problems, nerve damage, or death. Malpractice includes administering too much or too little anesthesia, failing to monitor vital signs, not reviewing patient history for allergies or interactions, or improper intubation. Both anesthesiologists and nurse anesthetists can be liable. These cases often involve severe injuries requiring substantial compensation for lifetime care.
What if multiple doctors were involved in my care? +
When multiple healthcare providers are involved, determining liability requires careful analysis. Each provider who breached the standard of care can be held liable for their portion of fault. California follows comparative fault rules, apportioning liability among defendants. This can actually benefit you under new MICRA rules, as separate caps may apply to different categories of defendants, potentially tripling available non-economic damages.
Can I sue for plastic surgery gone wrong? +
Cosmetic surgery malpractice follows the same legal standards as other medical procedures. Claims arise from surgical errors, failure to obtain informed consent about risks, performing unnecessary procedures, or causing disfigurement. Even elective procedures require meeting the standard of care. Common issues include asymmetry, scarring, nerve damage, infections, or results drastically different from what was promised. Document all before/after conditions.
How do I prove my doctor was negligent? +
Proving negligence requires showing the doctor's care fell below accepted medical standards. We use medical records, expert testimony, medical literature, hospital policies, and witness statements. Expert witnesses in the same specialty testify about what a reasonable doctor would have done differently. We also investigate patterns of similar errors, prior complaints, and systemic failures. Our team handles all aspects of building proof while you focus on recovery.
What if the hospital says it's not responsible for the doctor's mistake? +
Hospitals often claim doctors are independent contractors to avoid liability. However, hospitals can still be liable under theories of apparent authority, non-delegable duties, or corporate negligence. If the hospital controlled the doctor's work, held them out as employees, or had its own negligent policies, they may be liable. We investigate the true relationship and pursue all viable theories to maximize recovery sources.
Can I sue for unnecessary surgery? +
Performing unnecessary surgery is malpractice if it deviates from accepted medical standards. This includes surgeries based on misdiagnosis, financially motivated procedures, or operations where conservative treatment was appropriate. You can recover for the surgical risks you unnecessarily faced, complications, recovery time, costs, and emotional distress. These cases often involve reviewing whether proper diagnostic steps were taken before recommending surgery.
What if I was discharged too early from the hospital? +
Premature discharge can be malpractice if it causes harm. Hospitals motivated by insurance pressure or bed shortages sometimes discharge patients who need continued care. If you suffered complications, readmission, or worsening condition due to early discharge, you may have a claim. We examine discharge criteria, vital signs, test results, and whether proper follow-up was arranged to determine if discharge was negligent.
Can I sue for misdiagnosis of a heart attack? +
Heart attack misdiagnosis is unfortunately common and often fatal. Symptoms dismissed as anxiety, indigestion, or musculoskeletal pain can be heart attacks requiring immediate treatment. Failure to perform EKGs, cardiac enzyme tests, or recognize symptoms constitutes malpractice. Women and younger patients are particularly at risk for misdiagnosis. Delayed treatment causes permanent heart damage or death that could have been prevented.
What if my child was injured by medical malpractice? +
Parents can file claims on behalf of injured children. Pediatric malpractice cases often involve birth injuries, misdiagnosis of serious conditions, medication errors (dosing errors are common in children), or failure to recognize developmental problems. Children may have extended statute of limitations - typically until their 8th birthday for injuries occurring before age 6. These cases often involve lifetime care needs requiring aggressive pursuit of maximum compensation.
Can I sue if my doctor failed to refer me to a specialist? +
Failure to refer to specialists when indicated can be malpractice. Primary care doctors must recognize when conditions exceed their expertise and require specialist care. Delayed referrals for cancer, heart disease, neurological conditions, or other serious illnesses can allow conditions to worsen. We examine whether a reasonable physician would have made the referral sooner and how the delay impacted your outcome.
What if I developed bedsores in the hospital? +
Bedsores (pressure ulcers) are often preventable and may indicate neglect. Hospitals and nursing homes must regularly reposition immobile patients, provide proper nutrition, maintain skin hygiene, and use pressure-relieving devices. Severe bedsores can cause infections, require surgery, or be fatal. Stage 3 or 4 bedsores are considered "never events" that should not occur with proper care. Document all sores with photographs.
Can I sue for wrong diagnosis that led to unnecessary treatment? +
Yes, incorrect diagnosis leading to unnecessary treatment is actionable malpractice. You can recover for harm from unneeded surgeries, chemotherapy, radiation, or medications. This includes physical side effects, emotional trauma, medical costs, lost wages, and the progression of your actual condition while receiving wrong treatment. These cases often involve significant damages as unnecessary treatments can cause serious permanent harm.
What if the hospital lost my test results? +
Lost or mishandled test results can constitute malpractice if it causes diagnostic delays or improper treatment. Hospitals have duties to properly maintain and communicate test results. Lost results requiring repeat testing, delaying diagnosis, or preventing proper treatment create liability. We investigate whether proper procedures were followed and how the loss impacted your care. Electronic health record systems make "lost" results increasingly suspicious.
Can I sue if my surgery was performed on the wrong body part? +
Wrong-site surgery is a "never event" that constitutes clear malpractice. This includes operating on the wrong side, wrong body part, or wrong patient entirely. These errors violate basic safety protocols like surgical timeouts and site marking. Liability is typically clear-cut, focusing on damages including the harm from unnecessary surgery, cost of corrective procedures, and the ongoing effects of not having the intended surgery performed correctly.
What if a medical student or resident caused my injury? +
Teaching hospitals can be liable for injuries caused by residents, interns, or medical students. Supervising physicians have duties to properly oversee trainees. Patients must consent to treatment by trainees, and supervision must be adequate for the procedure's complexity. Both the trainee, supervising physician, and hospital may be liable. These cases often involve questions about whether supervision was adequate and whether you were properly informed.
Can I sue for injuries during clinical trials? +
Clinical trial injuries may give rise to malpractice claims despite informed consent. Researchers must follow protocols, properly screen participants, monitor for adverse events, and provide appropriate care when problems arise. Failure to disclose risks, protocol violations, or inadequate monitoring can create liability. While some risk is inherent in trials, negligence in conducting the trial is actionable. Review your consent carefully with an attorney.
What if I was injured during a telemedicine appointment? +
Telemedicine providers must meet the same standard of care as in-person treatment. Malpractice can occur through misdiagnosis due to technology limitations, failure to recommend in-person examination when necessary, prescribing errors, or inadequate follow-up. California has specific telemedicine regulations. These cases involve unique challenges but increasing precedent as telemedicine expands. Document all virtual visits and communications carefully.
Can I sue if my medical records were altered? +
Altering medical records is serious misconduct that can strengthen your malpractice case. Electronic records leave audit trails showing changes. Suspicious late entries, inconsistencies, or missing records suggest consciousness of guilt. Beyond supporting malpractice claims, record alteration may constitute fraud warranting punitive damages. We use forensic techniques to detect alterations and preserve evidence of tampering.
What if the doctor didn't listen to my concerns? +
Dismissing patient concerns can constitute malpractice if it leads to missed diagnoses or delayed treatment. Doctors must take patient-reported symptoms seriously and investigate appropriately. This is particularly important for women and minorities who studies show are more likely to have concerns dismissed. Document all attempts to report symptoms and the provider's responses. Patient advocacy failures often contribute to diagnostic errors.
Can I sue for psychiatric malpractice? +
Psychiatric malpractice includes medication errors, failure to prevent suicide in high-risk patients, improper involuntary commitment, boundary violations, or misdiagnosis of serious mental conditions. These cases require specialized expertise and sensitivity. Damages can include worsening mental health, physical harm from medications, loss of liberty from improper commitment, or tragedy from failure to properly assess suicide risk. Documentation of treatment and symptoms is crucial.
What should I do immediately after discovering medical malpractice? +
First, seek appropriate medical treatment for your condition from a different provider. Document everything: symptoms, conversations with providers, and impacts on your life. Request copies of all medical records immediately. Take photographs of visible injuries. Keep a journal of your recovery. Don't sign anything from the hospital or insurance companies. Contact our attorneys immediately - early investigation is crucial. Never confront the provider about potential malpractice without legal counsel.
Will filing a malpractice lawsuit affect my ability to get healthcare? +
Healthcare providers cannot legally refuse treatment because you filed a malpractice claim. Your lawsuit is confidential during litigation and becomes public record only if it goes to trial. Most providers won't know about your claim unless you tell them. We can help you find appropriate new providers if needed. Your health and access to quality care remain paramount - pursuing justice shouldn't compromise your healthcare.
