What to Do After a California Bus Accident

Critical steps to protect your rights and maximize compensation - especially with the 6-month government claim deadline

1

Ensure Safety & Call 911

Move to safety if possible and call emergency services immediately. Bus accidents often involve multiple injuries. Get medical attention even if you feel fine - many bus accident injuries like traumatic brain injuries and spinal damage may not show symptoms immediately.

2

Document Everything

Take photos of the bus, accident scene, your injuries, and any hazards. Get the bus number, driver's information, and route details. Collect witness contact information. If it's an MTA, school, or government bus, this evidence is crucial for your claim.

3

Report to Proper Authority

File a police report immediately. For MTA or government buses, you must file a government claim within 6 MONTHS - not the standard 2 years. Missing this deadline can eliminate your right to compensation entirely.

4

Seek Medical Treatment

Get comprehensive medical evaluation immediately. Bus accidents commonly cause whiplash, traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord injuries, and PTSD. Document all treatments - insurance companies will use any gap in treatment against you.

5

Don't Give Statements

Never give recorded statements to bus company representatives or their insurers without an attorney. They're trained to minimize claims. Even saying "I'm okay" can be used to deny your compensation later.

6

Contact Bus Accident Attorney

Common California Bus Accident Injuries

Understanding the severe and often life-changing injuries from bus crashes

Traumatic Brain Injuries (TBI)

Bus accidents are a leading cause of traumatic brain injuries. Without seatbelts, passengers' heads strike windows, poles, seats, or floors during collisions. Even "mild" TBIs (concussions) can cause lasting problems with memory, concentration, mood changes, and cognitive function. Moderate to severe TBIs may result in permanent disability, personality changes, seizures, or require 24/7 care. Symptoms may not appear immediately - some victims seem fine after the accident only to develop serious problems days or weeks later. The lifetime cost of treating severe TBI can exceed millions of dollars.

Spinal Cord Injuries

The violent forces in bus accidents can fracture, dislocate, or compress vertebrae, damaging the delicate spinal cord. Injuries range from herniated discs causing chronic pain to complete severing resulting in paralysis. Incomplete injuries may cause partial paralysis, loss of sensation, or impaired organ function. Complete injuries result in total loss of function below the injury level - quadriplegia from neck injuries or paraplegia from lower back injuries. Victims face enormous medical expenses, home modifications, specialized equipment, and lifetime care needs.

Whiplash and Neck Injuries

The sudden jerking motion in bus accidents commonly causes whiplash - soft tissue damage to neck muscles, ligaments, and tendons. While often dismissed as "minor," whiplash can cause chronic pain, headaches, dizziness, and reduced range of motion lasting months or years. Some victims develop chronic pain syndrome requiring ongoing treatment. The lack of headrests on many bus seats increases whiplash severity.

Broken Bones and Fractures

Passengers thrown during bus accidents commonly suffer fractures to arms, legs, ribs, hips, and facial bones. Elderly passengers face particular risk of hip fractures, which can be life-threatening. Complex fractures may require multiple surgeries, metal implants, and extensive rehabilitation. Some victims never fully recover mobility or strength. Facial fractures can cause permanent disfigurement requiring reconstructive surgery.

Internal Injuries

The force of bus accidents can cause internal bleeding, organ damage, and punctured lungs without visible external injuries. Spleen lacerations, liver damage, kidney injuries, and internal bleeding may not show symptoms immediately but can be life-threatening. These injuries require emergency surgery and intensive care, with potential long-term complications affecting organ function.

Psychological Trauma and PTSD

Bus accident trauma extends beyond physical injuries. Many victims develop post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), experiencing flashbacks, nightmares, and severe anxiety about transportation. Some can never ride buses again, impacting their ability to work or maintain independence. Depression, anxiety disorders, and phobias are common, requiring long-term therapy and medication. Children are particularly vulnerable to lasting psychological effects.

Soft Tissue Injuries

Beyond whiplash, bus accidents cause numerous soft tissue injuries including torn ligaments, strained muscles, and damaged tendons throughout the body. These injuries cause chronic pain, reduced mobility, and may require extensive physical therapy. Insurance companies often minimize soft tissue injuries, but they can significantly impact quality of life and ability to work.

Facial and Dental Injuries

Passengers thrown forward may strike poles, seats, or windows, causing facial lacerations, broken teeth, jaw fractures, and eye injuries. These injuries often require multiple reconstructive surgeries, dental implants, and may cause permanent scarring or disfigurement affecting self-esteem and employment opportunities.

Burns

Though less common, bus fires or explosions following accidents can cause severe burns requiring skin grafts, multiple surgeries, and leaving permanent scarring. Burn victims face excruciating pain, infection risks, and long rehabilitation periods. The psychological impact of disfigurement adds to their suffering.

Crush Injuries and Amputations

In severe bus accidents, particularly rollovers, passengers may suffer crush injuries when trapped under seats or debris. These injuries can cause compartment syndrome, requiring emergency surgery to prevent tissue death. In worst cases, limbs must be amputated. Victims face lifetime disability, prosthetic costs, and career changes.

Who's Liable in California Bus Accidents?

Multiple parties may be responsible for your injuries and damages

Bus Driver Liability

Bus drivers must exercise the "highest degree of care" under California's common carrier law. Any negligence including distracted driving (cell phone use), fatigued driving, speeding, failing to secure wheelchairs, sudden stops without warning, driving under the influence, inadequate passenger assistance, or violating traffic laws creates liability. Even minor mistakes that wouldn't create liability for regular drivers can make bus drivers responsible.

Bus Company/Transit Authority Liability

Whether MTA, school district, or private company, bus operators face liability for negligent hiring (failing background checks), inadequate driver training, pressuring drivers to skip breaks, poor vehicle maintenance, defective equipment, violating federal/state regulations, and failing to address known safety issues. Government entities like MTA can't hide behind sovereign immunity for operational negligence.

Government Entity Liability

Cities, counties, and state agencies operating buses face special rules. While they have some immunities, they're liable for dangerous road conditions they knew about, inadequate bus stop design, poor traffic signal timing, and failure to maintain safe routes. The 6-month claim deadline is strict - missing it usually bars your claim entirely.

Other Vehicle Drivers

When other vehicles cause bus accidents through drunk driving, distracted driving, running red lights, unsafe lane changes, or failing to yield, they're liable. However, bus companies often share liability for failing to avoid the accident. Multiple insurance policies may apply, increasing available compensation.

Maintenance Contractor Liability

Many bus companies outsource maintenance. These contractors face liability for inadequate inspections, using wrong parts, failing to fix known problems, falsifying maintenance records, or unqualified mechanics working on buses. Proving maintenance negligence requires examining detailed records.

Parts Manufacturer Liability

Defective bus parts including faulty brakes, defective tires, steering failures, door malfunctions, wheelchair lift defects, or seat defects create strict liability - you don't need to prove negligence, only that the defect caused injuries. Manufacturers have deep pockets but mount aggressive defenses.

School District Liability

School districts face liability for inadequate driver screening, failing to supervise students, dangerous loading zones, inadequate safety policies, ignoring driver complaints, and violating Education Code requirements. California law provides special protections for children but requires careful navigation of governmental immunity rules.

Tour/Charter Company Liability

Private bus companies often violate federal hours-of-service rules, pushing drivers beyond safe limits. They may use unsafe vehicles, hire unqualified drivers, or lack adequate insurance. Out-of-state companies create jurisdictional challenges requiring experienced counsel.

Property Owner Liability

Property owners where bus stops are located may face premises liability for dangerous conditions like inadequate lighting, broken pavement, missing railings, or ice/snow hazards. Shopping centers and schools must maintain safe boarding areas.

California Bus Accident FAQs

Get answers to over 50 common questions about bus accident claims

What's the deadline to file a bus accident claim in California?

+

The deadline depends on who operated the bus:

  • Government buses (MTA, school, city): You must file a government claim within 6 MONTHS of the accident. If denied, you have 6 months from denial to file a lawsuit.
  • Private buses: You generally have 2 years to file a lawsuit.
  • Federal vehicles: 2 years under the Federal Tort Claims Act.

Missing the 6-month government deadline usually eliminates your right to compensation entirely. Contact an attorney immediately to protect your rights.

What if I was standing when the bus crashed?

+

Standing passengers often suffer the most severe injuries since they have nothing to protect them during sudden stops or collisions. Common carriers must protect standing passengers by:

  • Avoiding sudden acceleration or braking when possible
  • Warning passengers before unavoidable sudden movements
  • Ensuring handrails and straps work properly
  • Not exceeding standing passenger capacity

The bus company can't blame you for standing when seats weren't available or when you were preparing to exit. You have strong grounds for compensation.

Can I sue if the bus driver slammed on brakes and I got hurt?

+

Yes. While drivers sometimes must brake suddenly for safety, they're liable if the sudden stop was due to:

  • Following too closely
  • Distracted driving
  • Speeding requiring emergency braking
  • Failing to warn passengers when possible
  • Poor route planning creating dangerous situations

Even if braking was necessary, the bus company may be liable if passengers weren't given adequate handholds or if the bus was overcrowded.

What damages can I recover from a California bus accident?

+

California bus accident victims can recover:

  • Medical expenses: Emergency care, surgery, medication, physical therapy, future treatments
  • Lost wages: Time missed from work, reduced earning capacity, lost benefits
  • Pain and suffering: Physical pain, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life
  • Property damage: Damaged belongings, clothing, electronics
  • Disability/disfigurement: Compensation for permanent injuries
  • Wrongful death: Funeral costs, lost support, loss of companionship
  • Punitive damages: For extremely reckless conduct (rare against government)

What's California's common carrier law for buses?

+

California Civil Code 2100 requires common carriers (including all buses) to use "utmost care and diligence" for passenger safety. This means:

  • Higher standard than regular "reasonable care"
  • Must do everything reasonably possible to prevent harm
  • Liable for even slight negligence
  • Must provide safe vehicles and competent drivers
  • Must protect passengers from other passengers' misconduct

This strict standard makes it easier to prove bus company liability compared to regular auto accidents.

Can I sue a school district for a school bus accident?

+

Yes, but special rules apply:

  • Must file government claim within 6 months
  • Districts have some governmental immunities
  • Cannot get punitive damages
  • Damage caps may apply in some situations
  • Must prove district negligence (not just driver error)

School districts are liable for inadequate driver training, poor supervision, dangerous routes, or failing to protect students. California law provides special protections for injured children.

What if I was partially at fault for my bus accident injuries?

+

California follows "pure comparative negligence," meaning you can recover even if partially at fault, but your compensation reduces by your fault percentage. However:

  • Common carriers' high duty often makes passenger fault minimal
  • Standing, walking to exit, or not holding handrails isn't necessarily negligent
  • Bus companies must account for normal passenger behavior
  • Children and disabled passengers held to different standards

Insurance companies exaggerate passenger fault to reduce payments. We fight these tactics to maximize your recovery.
