Types of Brain Injuries We Handle

Comprehensive representation for all TBI cases throughout California

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Caused by external force to the head from car accidents, falls, or blows. Ranges from mild concussions to severe injuries causing permanent disability. Even "mild" TBI can result in lasting cognitive and emotional changes requiring extensive treatment.

Concussion / Mild TBI

Often dismissed as "just a concussion," these injuries can cause persistent post-concussion syndrome with headaches, dizziness, memory problems, and mood changes lasting months or years. Multiple concussions increase risk of CTE.

Diffuse Axonal Injury

Severe rotational forces tear brain nerve fibers, often from high-speed crashes or violent shaking. Can cause coma, permanent vegetative state, or severe cognitive and physical disabilities. Requires lifetime care.

Anoxic/Hypoxic Brain Injury

Oxygen deprivation from drowning, medical malpractice, or cardiac arrest. Even brief oxygen loss causes permanent damage. Victims may suffer memory loss, cognitive impairment, and physical disabilities.

Penetrating Brain Injury

Object penetrates skull damaging brain tissue. Common in workplace accidents, violent crimes, and severe crashes. Often requires multiple surgeries and causes permanent focal deficits.

Coup-Contrecoup Injury

Brain impacts skull on impact side (coup) and opposite side (contrecoup) from violent motion. Common in vehicle accidents and falls. Causes damage to multiple brain areas with complex symptoms.

What is considered a traumatic brain injury in California law?

+
Under California law, a traumatic brain injury is any disruption of normal brain function caused by external physical force. This includes concussions, contusions, penetrating injuries, and anoxic injuries from lack of oxygen. Even "mild" TBIs without loss of consciousness qualify if they cause cognitive, physical, or psychological symptoms. California courts recognize that brain injuries can occur without direct head impact through acceleration-deceleration forces. Medical documentation of altered mental state, confusion, memory loss, or any period of unconsciousness establishes TBI for legal purposes.

How long after an accident can brain injury symptoms appear?

+
Brain injury symptoms can appear immediately or develop over days, weeks, or even months after trauma. Adrenaline and shock often mask initial symptoms. Delayed symptoms include headaches developing days later, memory problems emerging weeks post-injury, personality changes noticed by family over time, and depression or anxiety developing months later. Slow brain bleeds may not cause symptoms for weeks. This is why immediate medical evaluation is crucial even without obvious symptoms. California's discovery rule may extend the statute of limitations if symptoms appear later, but prompt medical attention strengthens your claim.

Do I have a case if my CT scan or MRI was normal?

+
Yes, you may still have a valid brain injury case even with normal imaging. Many brain injuries, especially mild TBIs and concussions, don't show on standard CT scans or MRIs. Microscopic damage to neurons and axons isn't visible on routine imaging. California courts recognize that normal scans don't rule out brain injury. Advanced testing like DTI MRI, PET scans, or neuropsychological evaluations can document injuries standard tests miss. Symptoms, clinical examinations, and cognitive testing often prove brain injury when imaging appears normal. Never let insurance companies dismiss your injury based solely on normal initial scans.

What are the warning signs I need immediate medical attention?

+
Seek emergency care immediately for: worsening headache, repeated vomiting, seizures, inability to wake up, one pupil larger than the other, slurred speech, weakness or numbness in limbs, unusual behavior or confusion, loss of consciousness even briefly, clear fluids draining from nose or ears, or convulsions. For children, also watch for persistent crying, refusal to eat, or changes in sleep patterns. These symptoms may indicate brain bleeding, swelling, or other complications requiring immediate treatment. Delaying care can be life-threatening and may also impact your legal claim.

Can a concussion cause permanent damage?

+
Yes, concussions can cause permanent damage, especially with inadequate treatment or multiple injuries. Post-concussion syndrome can persist for years with chronic headaches, memory problems, difficulty concentrating, mood changes, and sleep disturbances. Multiple concussions increase risk of chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), early dementia, and depression. Studies show 15-30% of concussion victims have symptoms lasting over a year. California law recognizes long-term concussion effects for compensation purposes. Never minimize a concussion as "just a bump on the head" - proper treatment and legal representation are essential.

+
California generally allows two years from the injury date to file a personal injury lawsuit for brain injuries. However, important exceptions apply: The discovery rule may extend the deadline if brain injury symptoms weren't immediately apparent. Minors have until two years after their 18th birthday. Government claims require filing within six months. Medical malpractice cases have different deadlines. If the defendant leaves California, the statute may be tolled. Never wait to consult an attorney - evidence disappears, witnesses forget, and insurance companies use delays against you. We can file protective claims while investigating your case.

Appeals don't retry facts - appellate courts review legal issues only. Process takes 12-18 months and is expensive. Success rates are low (approximately 20%). New trials are rare remedies. Better strategy is thorough trial preparation preventing appealable errors. We protect the record throughout trial, preserving appeal rights while minimizing need. Most brain injury plaintiffs who lose at trial don't pursue appeals due to cost and low success probability. Strong case preparation remains best approach.

What if I had a pre-existing condition?

+
Pre-existing conditions don't prevent recovery under California's "eggshell plaintiff" rule - defendants take victims as they find them. You're entitled to compensation for aggravation of pre-existing conditions and new injuries regardless of prior vulnerabilities. Insurance companies aggressively investigate medical history seeking pre-existing conditions to blame for current symptoms. We distinguish new injuries from prior conditions using medical records showing stable pre-existing conditions before accident, expert testimony on aggravation versus progression, and comparative imaging showing new damage. Even if you had prior concussions, migraines, or depression, you deserve compensation for worsening. Vulnerability doesn't diminish defendant liability. We turn pre-existing conditions into strengths showing why injuries are particularly devastating for you.

Can I get compensation for PTSD after brain injury?

+
Yes, PTSD frequently accompanies traumatic brain injuries and warrants separate compensation. Trauma from accidents causing TBI often triggers PTSD with flashbacks, nightmares, anxiety, avoidance behaviors, and hypervigilance. Brain injuries can impair emotional regulation, worsening PTSD. Compensation covers psychiatric treatment, medications, therapy (including EMDR and CBT), lost wages from psychological symptoms, and impact on relationships and daily life. PTSD is diagnosed through psychological evaluation separate from TBI assessment. Insurance companies often dismiss psychological injuries as less valid than physical injuries. We document PTSD through psychiatric records, psychological testing, and testimony about life impact. Combined TBI and PTSD significantly increases case value recognizing dual trauma burden.

What if the brain injury victim dies?

+
Families can pursue wrongful death and survival claims when brain injuries prove fatal. Wrongful death compensates families for funeral expenses, lost financial support, loss of companionship and guidance, and household services value. Survival claims recover victim's damages before death including medical expenses, lost wages, and pain and suffering. California's two-year statute applies from date of death. Eligible claimants include spouses, children, and sometimes parents or dependents. Brain injury deaths may occur immediately or months/years later from complications. Delayed deaths don't diminish claims if causally related. We handle these sensitive cases compassionately while aggressively pursuing accountability. Families deserve compensation for preventable deaths and devastating losses.

Defendants cannot use immigration status to avoid liability or reduce compensation. Veterans can pursue civilian claims for brain injuries occurring outside military service. While active duty injuries are limited to VA benefits, veterans can sue for civilian accidents, medical malpractice at non-VA facilities, motor vehicle accidents, and other civilian incidents. VA disability ratings don't prevent civilian claims but may affect damage calculations. Civilian compensation can supplement VA benefits providing fuller recovery. Veterans often have pre-existing TBI from service making them vulnerable to additional injuries. We understand military culture, work with VA healthcare providers, and maximize combined benefits. How do you prove liability in brain injury cases?

+
Proving liability requires establishing the defendant owed you a duty of care (drivers must operate safely, property owners maintain premises), breached that duty through negligence or recklessness, causation linking breach to your brain injury, and damages resulting from the injury. Evidence includes police reports, witness testimony, surveillance footage, expert accident reconstruction, vehicle black box data, maintenance records, safety violations, and toxicology results. Different cases require specific proof - product liability needs defect evidence, premises liability requires notice of hazards, medical malpractice needs expert testimony on standard of care. Our investigation uncovers all evidence supporting liability. Former defense experience reveals what evidence defendants fear most.
